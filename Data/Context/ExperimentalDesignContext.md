# Study Context: Evaluating Whether Stress History Impacts the Zebrafish Gut Microbiome’s Resistance and Resiliency to Environmental Stressors

## Overview
This study investigates how **historical stress exposure**—previous encounters with environmental stressors—shapes both **host** and **microbiome** responses to new perturbations and their recovery afterward. The central concept is **historical contingency** within host–microbiome systems: past experiences influence future stability, resistance, and resilience.

---

## Model System
- **Organism:** Adult zebrafish (*Danio rerio*, 5D strain)
- **Total fish:** 720 individuals
- **Replicates:** 24 tanks (replicated design)
- **Rationale:** Zebrafish serve as a tractable vertebrate model for studying gut microbiome dynamics, environmental stress, and host–microbe interactions.

---

## Experimental Treatments
Three sequential stressors were applied over 60 days, simulating cumulative environmental history:

1. **Antibiotic Exposure (Days 0–14)**  
   - Delivered via medicated gelatin feed containing **streptomycin, ciprofloxacin, and ampicillin** (50 µg g⁻¹ body weight per day).  
   - Purpose: Perturb the gut microbiome and test how prior microbial disruption affects later responses.

2. **Heat Stress (Days 15–29)**  
   - Water temperature gradually raised from 28 °C to 35 °C over 3 days, maintained for 15 days, then lowered back to 28 °C.  
   - Purpose: Apply an abiotic environmental stressor after antibiotic exposure.

3. **Parasite Exposure (Days 30–60)**  
   - Continuous exposure to *Pseudocapillaria tomentosa* eggs via a flow-through effluent manifold from infected donor tanks.  
   - Purpose: Introduce a biotic challenge following one or more historical stressors.

Each fish experienced one of eight possible combinations of these treatments, forming a **fully factorial design**:

A± × T± × P± → Eight Exposure Regimes

## Experimental Timeline

| Phase | Days | Stressor | Objective |
|:------|:----:|:----------|:-----------|
| 1 | 0–14 | Antibiotics | Initial microbial disturbance |
| 2 | 15–29 | Elevated temperature | Secondary abiotic stress |
| 3 | 30–60 | Parasite infection | Biotic challenge and recovery |

---

## Sampling Design
- **Sampling points:** Day 0 (baseline), Day 14, Day 29, Day 60 (final).  
- **Collected samples:**
  - **Fecal and intestinal samples** for gut microbiome sequencing (16S rRNA).  
  - **Intestinal tissue** for host transcriptomics (RNA-Seq; subset of 72 fish at Day 60).  
- **Sample size:** 3 fish per tank per sampling point (n = 72 per phase).

---

## Measured Outcomes
- **Host physiology:**  
  - Mortality rates  
  - Infection prevalence and worm burden  

- **Gut microbiome:**  
  - Alpha and beta diversity changes  
  - Compositional displacement and recovery  
  - Taxon-specific abundance shifts  

- **Host–microbiome integration:**  
  - Correlations between host gene expression (DEGs) and microbial taxa (DATs)  
  - Identification of taxa linked to health (e.g., *Culicoidibacter*) or disease risk (*Tundrisphaera*, *Cloacibacterium*)

---

## Key Design Features
- **Sequential (not simultaneous) exposures** model realistic cumulative stress histories.  
- Incorporates both **abiotic** (antibiotics, heat) and **biotic** (parasite) stressors.  
- Eight unique treatment combinations allow assessment of stress-specific and cumulative effects.  
- **Day 60 endpoint** captures both perturbation effects and recovery dynamics.  
- Integrates physiological, microbiome, and transcriptomic data to evaluate host–microbiome coordination.

---

## Summary Statement
In total, the study reared 720 adult zebrafish under combinations of antibiotic, heat, and parasite exposure in a **three-phase sequential design** to test how prior stress history shapes mortality, infection outcomes, gut microbiome diversity and composition, host intestinal gene expression, and overall host–microbiome resilience.

This file provides essential context for any follow-up questions about experimental design, interpretation of results, or modeling of host–microbiome stability under sequential environmental stress.
